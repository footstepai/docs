---
title: 'find_and_route'
description: 'Find a place and get directions to it in one step'
---

Geocode a destination (address, place name, or landmark) and route to it from your current location. Combines geocoding and routing in a single call so the agent doesn't need to chain two separate tools.

## Example prompts

- "How do I get to the nearest train station from here?"
- "Directions to Buckingham Palace from Kings Cross"
- "Walking route to the British Museum from my hotel at 51.52, -0.13"
- "Find 10 Downing Street and show me how to cycle there"

## What you get back

The geocoded destination (label, coordinates, country code, place type, confidence), the route (distance, duration, terrain, turn-by-turn steps), and up to three alternative destination matches when the top result wasn't unambiguous.

Alternatives are filtered for usefulness: same country as the primary, above a minimum confidence threshold, and not broader admin areas that already contain the primary (e.g. "London" isn't returned as an alternative to "Tower Bridge"). Surviving alternatives are sorted by distance from the primary so the most spatially plausible candidates surface first. The geocoder is also biased toward the caller's `origin`, so a "Tower Bridge" search from London prefers the actual bridge over namesakes elsewhere.

Pass `narrative: true` for an AI-generated 1-3 sentence summary of the route alongside the structured data. When narrative is included, the response also carries `narrative_units` (`kilometers` or `miles`) telling you which units the summary used. All numeric route fields are always in meters / seconds regardless.

## Tips for landmark destinations

- **Qualify with city context** — `"Big Ben, London"` resolves more reliably than `"Big Ben"`.
- **Plain street addresses don't need extra context.**

## Rendering on a map: `include_geometry`

By default this tool returns a lean response — the encoded polyline of the route and the sample-by-sample elevation profile are stripped out so standalone LLM agents (which can't render maps) don't pay for them in tokens.

If your client renders results on a map (Mapbox, MapLibre, Leaflet, etc.), pass `include_geometry: true`. The response then carries the route polyline and elevation profile per leg alongside the same summary fields. The map renders the geometry; the LLM still reasons over the summary.

Setting `include_geometry: true` on every call from a map-rendering client is tedious. See the [getting-started guide](/mcp/getting-started#map-rendering-clients) for a one-line Vercel AI SDK middleware that injects the flag automatically.
